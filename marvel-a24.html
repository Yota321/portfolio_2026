01 — Concept

TheQuestion.

This project started as a question rather than a brief. What if Marvel stories were told with A24's restraint, intimacy, and emotional weight instead of spectacle-first blockbuster energy?

Instead of scale, explosions, and franchise continuity, I imagined Marvel films as character-driven stories. Slower pacing, quieter visuals, themes built around grief, identity, and internal conflict.

The goal was not to replace Marvel's identity but to reinterpret it through a completely different cinematic lens. One cohesive visual universe across posters, merchandise, and platform.

Marvel's Register
Scale, Spectacle, Franchise

Hero poses, explosive action, continuity-first storytelling designed for maximum visual impact at cinema scale.

A24's Register
Intimacy, Grief, Presence

Character as atmosphere. Negative space. Themes of identity and internal conflict told through restraint rather than explosion.

Visual direction · tone, palette, and cinematic reference
02 — Approach

Brand andCampaign.

I approached this as a brand and campaign exercise rather than a single poster or artwork. The focus was on consistency across tone, typography, imagery, and storytelling across every format.

Promotional material, posters, merchandise, platform visuals: all had to feel like they came from the same studio. Remove the Marvel name and it should still feel like one world.

Brand Tone
Positioning and Voice

Restrained, emotional, literary. Where Marvel is loud, this version is quiet. Where Marvel announces, this version suggests.

Film Poster System
Standalone Identities

Each poster designed as its own film identity within a shared visual language, not variants of a single template.

Merchandise
Narrative Extensions

Objects that feel cinematic and collectible rather than promotional. Each item carries the film's identity, not just its logo.

Platform
Streaming Presentation

How tone translates beyond print, into thumbnails, hierarchy, and first impressions on a streaming interface.

03 — Poster System

PosterSeries.

The posters were designed to feel minimal, emotional, and slightly unsettling. No action shots. No character poses. The focus was entirely on atmosphere, symbolism, and negative space.

Each poster treats the character as a presence rather than a hero. The image should create feeling before it communicates plot. That is the A24 principle applied to a Marvel property.

04 — Merchandise

PhysicalExtensions.

Merchandise was designed as an extension of the film's identity rather than fan service. Each item carries the same restrained visual language as the posters, with focus on texture, typography, and tone.

The intention was to create objects that feel cinematic and collectible, not promotional. Something you would find in an A24 merch drop, not a theme park gift shop.

Merchandise · t-shirt and steelbook as narrative extensions of the film identity
05 — Platform

StreamingExperience.

To explore how this reimagined universe would live digitally, I designed a conceptual Netflix-style interface. The question was whether tone could survive the transition from poster to screen.

Darker thumbnails, serif type in place of the standard sans, and an overall atmosphere closer to prestige drama than superhero blockbuster. The UI had to feel like a different kind of Marvel.

Streaming platform · conceptual Netflix-style interface with Odinson selected
06 — Motion

OpeningSequence.

The final piece was a short motion work imagining how a film from this universe would open. Designed to test pacing, transitions, and whether the visual identity held up in motion.

The Odinson sequence uses slow dissolves, grain, and atmospheric tone to establish character before a single word of dialogue. No title card. No fanfare. The opposite of a Marvel intro.

Motion · Odinson opening sequence
07 — Reflection

LookingBack.

This was a conceptual exploration, not an official collaboration. It was created to test how branding, tone, and storytelling behave across formats when the source material is familiar but the lens is completely different.

The most useful constraint was treating every decision as a studio decision rather than a design exercise. What would this studio greenlight? What would they never make? That framing kept the work consistent in a way that mood boards alone cannot.

What I would push further: a full title sequence with original sound design, and the complete opening two minutes of Odinson built as a short film concept. The motion side of this project has the most room to grow.
